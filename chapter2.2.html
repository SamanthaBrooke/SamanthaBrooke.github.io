The Death of Paper
Chapter Two

The way that we are able to publish now days, calls into question how technology has violently altered printed books in a way from which there is no return. We are so disconnected from the means of production that nobody seems to be aware that books are produced very differently then they were 100 years ago. Digital files are exchanged between writers, publishers and printers all over the world, and printing has become a far more instantaneous practice.16 Gone are the days of slaving over a printing press and hand carving woodblock letters. These days, people was instant gratification.

So should we be defending technology and e-books, noting them as a viable and improved evolution? Or is this notion seemingly retrograde, considering the essence of book production has been altered so dramatically? The very advent of electricty sparked this new found capacity for a digital world, that would unquestionably develop. Have such advancements in technology perhaps become a hinderence, making us ignorant to the beauty found in books printed on paper?

“Maybe nobody will care about printed books 50 years from now, but I do. When I read a book, I’m handling a specific object in a specific time and place. The fact that when I take the book off the shelf it still says the same thing - that’s reassuring… and he goes on … Someone worked really hard to make the language just right, just the way they wanted it. They were so sure of it that they printed it in ink, on paper. A screen always feels like we could delete that, change that, move it around. So for a literature-crazed person like me, it’s just not permanent enough.”17
